
# Speech Script for Presentation on Geoffrey Hinton (Duration: ~3 minutes)

**Slide 1 – Introduction**

Hello everyone and thank you for being here.  Today I want to take you through the life and work of Geoffrey Hinton, a scientist often called the godfather of modern artificial intelligence.  We will look at his early influences, the main ideas he introduced, the milestones of his career and how his contributions shaped the field.

---

**Slide 2 – Background & Early Life**

Hinton was born in London in 1947 into a family of scientists and artists.  He studied experimental psychology at Cambridge and later earned a Ph.D. in artificial intelligence.  He wanted to understand how the brain learns and believed that machines might learn in similar ways.  This interest drew him to **artificial neural networks**—computer models loosely inspired by neurons.  In the early 1980s many researchers were sceptical of neural nets.  Hinton moved to Canada partly because U.S. funding for AI was tied to defence projects; he sought academic freedom to explore his unconventional ideas.

---

**Slide 3 – Key Ideas & Challenges**

Hinton produced several technical breakthroughs.  In 1983 he introduced the **Boltzmann machine**, a neural network that learns by finding patterns in data.  Three years later he co‑authored the backpropagation paper, which showed how networks can learn by adjusting connections based on errorshttps://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/2024/hinton/facts/#:~:text=Prize%20motivation%3A%20%E2%80%9Cfor%20foundational%20discoveries,learning%20with%20artificial%20neural%20networks%E2%80%9D.  This method remains at the heart of deep learning today.  He also explored **distributed representations**—storing information across many units—which paved the way for modern language models.  

These achievements came despite strong headwinds: the AI community at the time preferred rule‑based systems and funding for neural networks was scarce.  Hinton rejected military funding and often worked with small groups of students.  His persistence kept neural networks alive when few believed in them.

---

**Slide 4 – Career Highlights**

After the Boltzmann machine (1983) and the backpropagation paper (1986), Hinton’s students Alex Krizhevsky and Ilya Sutskever built **AlexNet** in 2012.  This deep neural network won the ImageNet competition and proved that neural networks could outperform other methodshttps://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geoffrey_Hinton#:~:text=With%20David%20Rumelhart%20%20and,22.  A year later Google bought their company, DNNresearch, and Hinton split his time between the University of Toronto and Google Brain.  In 2018 he shared the **Turing Award** with Yann LeCun and Yoshua Bengio for their work on deep learning.  In 2023 he left Google so he could speak freely about the potential risks of AI.  The following year he shared the **Nobel Prize in Physics** for theoretical advances that enabled neural networkshttps://www.britannica.com/biography/Geoffrey-Hinton#:~:text=In%20May%202023%20Hinton%20quit,regret%20his%20life%E2%80%99s%20work%20but.  This timeline shows how decades of quiet persistence eventually reshaped the field.

---

**Slide 5 – Impact & Legacy**

Hinton’s ideas underpin many AI applications we see today.  Neural networks trained by backpropagation power image recognition, speech assistants, translation tools and recommendation systems.  He mentored students who became leaders in AI, and he co‑founded institutes like the Vector Institute in Toronto.  He has been honoured with major awards, including the Turing Award and the Nobel Prize.  At the same time he has publicly raised concerns about how AI could be misused, calling for careful development and regulation.  

On this slide the right‑hand boxes highlight how his background spans psychology and computing, how he persisted despite scepticism and limited funding, and how he now advocates caution.  These facts tell us about the person behind the research without trying to dictate how we should feel about him.

---

**Slide 6 – Conclusion**

To conclude, Geoffrey Hinton’s story shows how curiosity and persistence can lead to breakthroughs.  His work on neural networks laid the foundations of deep learning and influenced technologies used worldwide.  He also reminds us that scientists need to think about the societal impact of their work.  I hope this overview leaves you with a clearer picture of Hinton’s role in AI and invites you to form your own view of where artificial intelligence is heading.

Thank you for your attention.
