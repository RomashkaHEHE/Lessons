
# Speech Script for Presentation on Geoffrey Hinton (Duration: ~4 minutes)

**Slide 1 – Introduction**

Hello everyone and thank you for joining me.  Over the next few minutes I’d like to introduce you to a quiet, persistent scientist whose ideas now power many of the devices and services we use every day.  We’re going to explore the life and impact of Geoffrey Hinton, often called one of the founding fathers of modern artificial intelligence.  I’ll share how his early curiosity led him down an unconventional path, what hurdles he faced, and why his story matters to anyone interested in technology or science.

---

**Slide 2 – Background & Early Life**

Geoffrey Hinton was born in London in 1947 into a family steeped in research and creativity.  He originally studied experimental psychology, asking, “How does the brain learn?”  This question pulled him toward building artificial systems that might learn in similar ways.  Instead of following the mainstream approach of symbolic rules, he embraced the unfashionable concept of **neural networks**—computer models loosely inspired by neurons.  Many colleagues doubted this approach, and when funding in the U.S. became tied to defence projects he moved to Canada to keep his work free from military influence.  This independence would shape his career.

---

**Slide 3 – Skills & Obstacles**

Hinton’s technical insights changed how machines learn.  In the early 1980s he developed the **Boltzmann machine**, borrowing ideas from physics to allow computers to discover patterns on their own.  A few years later he co‑authored the famous paper that popularised **backpropagation**, the learning algorithm at the heart of almost all modern AI.  He also explored **distributed representations**—encoding information across many small units—and pioneered time‑delay networks for sequences.

These breakthroughs didn’t happen in a vacuum.  Most researchers dismissed neural networks as a fad and funding was hard to find.  Hinton’s ethical stance—refusing military funding—made things harder.  Yet he persevered, convinced that teaching computers to learn like brains would unlock new possibilities.  His ability to bridge psychology and computer science was central to his success.

---

**Slide 4 – Career Highlights**

Hinton’s career is punctuated by milestones.  After the Boltzmann machine (1983) and backpropagation (1986), he kept pushing.  In 2012 his students Alex Krizhevsky and Ilya Sutskever built **AlexNet**, a deep neural network that crushed an image‑recognition contest and convinced the broader community that deep learning was practical.  A year later Google acquired their company, DNNresearch.  He went on to co‑found the Vector Institute in Toronto, and in 2018 he and collaborators Yann LeCun and Yoshua Bengio received the **Turing Award** for their work on deep learning.  In 2023 he resigned from Google so he could speak openly about AI risks.  The following year he shared the **Nobel Prize in Physics** for foundational discoveries that enabled neural networks.  This timeline shows how decades of persistence eventually reshaped the field.

---

**Slide 5 – Impact & Inspiration**

Why does Hinton’s work matter to us?  The deep learning techniques he championed now power face recognition on your phone, speech assistants, language translation and recommendation systems.  By mentoring students like Krizhevsky, Sutskever and LeCun, he seeded a generation of researchers who went on to build companies such as Google Brain and OpenAI.  Hinton also stresses ethics: he warns that AI can be misused to create convincing fakes or displace workers, and he calls for responsible development.  His cross‑disciplinary approach, persistence in the face of scepticism and insistence on a moral compass are sources of inspiration for anyone tackling hard problems.

---

**Slide 6 – Conclusion**

To conclude, Geoffrey Hinton’s story illustrates how **persistence and curiosity** can reshape an entire field.  His ideas laid the foundations of deep learning, proving that machines could learn from data.  He built bridges between psychology and computer science and showed that collaboration across disciplines fuels innovation.  Finally, he reminds us that with great power comes responsibility: as we develop more capable AI systems we must consider their impacts on society.  I hope this brief journey through his life leaves you both informed and motivated to explore the world of artificial intelligence thoughtfully and ethically.

Thank you for your attention.
