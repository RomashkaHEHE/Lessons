
# Speech Script for Presentation on Geoffrey Hinton (Duration: ~4 minutes)

**Slide 1 – Introduction**

Hello everyone. Today I’d like to introduce you to Geoffrey Hinton, a British‑Canadian scientist who is often called the **“godfather of artificial intelligence.”**  Over the next few minutes I will share the story of his journey, the ideas he championed and how his work transformed the modern world.  By the end you’ll understand why this soft‑spoken researcher captured the world’s attention, and why his story matters to us today.

---

**Slide 2 – Background & Early Life**

Geoffrey Hinton was born in London in 1947 to a family steeped in sciencehttps://www.britannica.com/biography/Geoffrey-Hinton#:~:text=Geoffrey%20Hinton%20,Hopfield.  His parents and even his ancestors were researchers, so curiosity was part of his upbringing.  After studying psychology at university, he found himself fascinated by the question **“How does the brain learn?”**  Instead of following the mainstream path of symbolic logic, Hinton embraced the then‑unfashionable idea of **neural networks**—computer models loosely inspired by neurons in the brain.  This choice wasn’t popular; many colleagues dismissed it as a dead end.  When funding in the United States began to flow from military agencies, Hinton decided to leave rather than compromise his valueshttps://www.britannica.com/biography/Geoffrey-Hinton#:~:text=In%201982%20Hinton%20joined%20the,groundwork%20for%20neural%20network%20development.  He moved to Canada to continue his research, free from defence funding, and found an academic home in Toronto.

---

**Slide 3 – Skills & Overcoming Obstacles**

Hinton’s technical breakthroughs changed how computers learn.  In the early 1980s he invented the **Boltzmann machine**, which borrowed ideas from physics to teach computers to recognise patterns without being explicitly programmedhttps://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/2024/hinton/facts/#:~:text=Prize%20motivation%3A%20%E2%80%9Cfor%20foundational%20discoveries,learning%20with%20artificial%20neural%20networks%E2%80%9D.  A few years later he and colleagues published a seminal paper that popularised the **backpropagation algorithm**, a method that allows multi‑layer networks to learn from their mistakes by adjusting internal connectionshttps://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geoffrey_Hinton#:~:text=With%20David%20Rumelhart%20%20and,22.  This procedure is the backbone of nearly all modern AI systems.  He also advanced concepts such as **distributed representations**—ways of encoding information across many small units—and **time‑delay neural networks** for sequence data.

These innovations didn’t come easily.  At the time, neural‑network research was ridiculed.  Funding was scarce, and Hinton’s insistence on ethical research meant turning down military grantshttps://www.britannica.com/biography/Geoffrey-Hinton#:~:text=In%201982%20Hinton%20joined%20the,groundwork%20for%20neural%20network%20development.  He persisted because he believed that teaching machines to learn like brains would unlock new possibilities.  His resilience in the face of scepticism and his ability to bridge psychology and computer science were essential to his success.

---

**Slide 4 – Career Highlights**

Hinton’s career is dotted with milestones.  After the Boltzmann machine (1983) and backpropagation (1986), he continued to push boundaries.  In 2012 his students Alex Krizhevsky and Ilya Sutskever built **AlexNet**, a deep neural network that crushed the ImageNet image‑recognition competition, beating the nearest competitor by a wide marginhttps://www.britannica.com/biography/Geoffrey-Hinton#:~:text=Hinton%20returned%20to%20the%20University,vice%20president%20and%20engineering%20fellow.  This victory convinced the broader research community that deep learning was practical.  Hinton and his students formed a start‑up called DNNresearch; Google acquired it a year laterhttps://www.britannica.com/biography/Geoffrey-Hinton#:~:text=Hinton%20returned%20to%20the%20University,vice%20president%20and%20engineering%20fellow.  

He co‑founded the Vector Institute in Toronto to support AI researchhttps://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geoffrey_Hinton#:~:text=Hinton%20received%20the%202018%20Turing,28%20%5D%5B%2029.  In 2018 he received the **Turing Award**, often called the “Nobel Prize of computing,” alongside Yoshua Bengio and Yann LeCun for their work on deep learninghttps://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geoffrey_Hinton#:~:text=Hinton%20received%20the%202018%20Turing,28%20%5D%5B%2029.  In 2023 he resigned from Google so he could speak freely about the risks of AIhttps://www.britannica.com/biography/Geoffrey-Hinton#:~:text=In%20May%202023%20Hinton%20quit,regret%20his%20life%E2%80%99s%20work%20but.  A year later he shared the **2024 Nobel Prize in Physics** with John Hopfield for foundational discoveries that made neural networks possiblehttps://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geoffrey_Hinton#:~:text=Hinton%20received%20the%202018%20Turing,28%20%5D%5B%2029.  These honours reflect not only his technical achievements but also the global importance of his ideas.

---

**Slide 5 – Impact & Inspiration**

Why does this matter to us?  Hinton’s work underpins many technologies we use every day.  **Deep learning** powers your phone’s face recognition, virtual assistants, translation services and even the recommendation algorithms on social mediahttps://www.britannica.com/biography/Geoffrey-Hinton#:~:text=Hinton%20returned%20to%20the%20University,vice%20president%20and%20engineering%20fellow.  By mentoring students like Krizhevsky, Sutskever and LeCun, he multiplied his influence and helped shape companies such as Google, OpenAI and DeepMind.  

Hinton also highlights the importance of ethics.  He warns that AI can be misused to generate convincing fake media or displace workershttps://www.britannica.com/biography/Geoffrey-Hinton#:~:text=In%20May%202023%20Hinton%20quit,regret%20his%20life%E2%80%99s%20work%20but.  He urges researchers and policymakers to think about safety as they develop more powerful systemshttps://www.britannica.com/biography/Geoffrey-Hinton#:~:text=In%20May%202023%20Hinton%20quit,regret%20his%20life%E2%80%99s%20work%20but.  His career shows that groundbreaking science can coexist with a strong moral compass.

For students and young scientists, Hinton’s journey is inspirational.  He combined insights from psychology and computer science to solve hard problems, persevered through decades of scepticism, and remained true to his values.  He reminds us that innovation often happens at the intersection of disciplines, and that patience and integrity matter as much as brilliance.

---

**Slide 6 – Conclusion**

In closing, Geoffrey Hinton’s story shows how one person’s curiosity and determination can reshape an entire field.  He pioneered the methods that make modern AI possible, mentored a new generation of researchers and, when deep learning finally took off, used his voice to call for caution.  His example challenges us to **think boldly, stay resilient in the face of doubt, collaborate across disciplines and always consider the broader impact of our work**.

Thank you for listening.  I hope this brief look at Geoffrey Hinton’s life leaves you not only informed but inspired to explore the incredible possibilities of artificial intelligence responsibly.
